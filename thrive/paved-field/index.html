The Paved Field

Data centers need land. Suburbs need land. Farmers have land. Guess who's losing.

Juan Camilo Echeverri — Executive Broker, PAK Home Realty · Rogers, AR
March 6, 2026 · United States · THRIVE
Listen to this article
1
Human Becoming

Tyler Oliver stands at the edge of a peanut field with an Amadas harvester kicking up dust behind him. He points to a tree line that wasn't always the edge. Behind it, rooftops. They're visible now — pale siding and pitched roofs where cotton grew five years ago.

He's twenty-seven. Fourth generation on this land outside Smithfield, Virginia. His great-grandfather cleared it. His grandfather irrigated it. His father diversified into peanuts and corn — figured that was the smart move, the way to keep going. Tyler doesn't farm alone. He attends board meetings. He sits on committees. He drives to Richmond and sometimes to Washington. He's already fighting for what his family spent a century building.

He doesn't use the word "advocacy." He calls it showing up.

"You look at that tree line and you think, that was always there. But it wasn't. The field was there. The field was first."

The neighborhoods don't arrive with a bulldozer. They arrive with a rezoning notice. Then a surveyor's truck. Then a foundation pour that turns a row crop into a cul-de-sac. By the time you see the rooftops, the soil is already gone.

Tyler's hands are calloused from harvest, but his evenings are spent reading county agendas. He doesn't talk about loss. He talks about time — how there used to be more of it, and how that changed.

2
Structural Read

What's happening around Tyler Oliver's farm is not a local anomaly. It's the leading edge of a national mechanism: a tech-and-housing building boom consuming American farmland at accelerating rates.[1]

The mechanism is economic arbitrage. Agricultural land is valued at production capacity — what a peanut or corn crop can yield per acre. Development land is valued at speculative or commercial potential. When a data center generates a $3 billion tax base increase for a town of 1,100 people, the economic incentive to convert farmland overwhelms anything agriculture can offer.

Mechanism Land-value arbitrage between agricultural use ($400/acre yield) and commercial development ($400,000/acre tax value) creates a structural incentive for conversion. The gap widens as AI infrastructure demand accelerates, making rural farmland the cheapest input for the most capital-intensive industry of the decade.

In Isle of Wight County, Virginia, population is projected to grow 12.5% — from 40,000 to 45,000 — over the next fifteen years. Tyler Oliver serves as president of the Virginia Grain Producers Association and as Virginia's delegate to the National Corn Growers Association, pushing for limits on development encroachment.[1]

In Ellendale, North Dakota — population 1,100 — Applied Digital is building a 400-megawatt AI data center on 219 annexed acres. The facility uses North Dakota's cold climate for natural cooling, saving an estimated $1 billion long-term. The town's tax base will increase roughly twentyfold.[2]

Al Schrader, an attorney at Roderick Linton Belfance LLP in Akron, Ohio, puts the acceleration bluntly: "You used to have 50 years to do what you have to do in about 10."[1]

"You used to have 50 years to do what you have to do in about 10." — Al Schrader, Roderick Linton Belfance LLP
Structural Tension The tension is not simply "development bad, farming good." Ellendale's data center brought generational economic change to a town that was shrinking. The question is governance: who decides which fields become servers, and whether farmers have seats at the table. Dutchess County, New York — where IBM arrived 70 years ago and disrupted Hudson Valley agriculture — now uses public-private partnerships, an agricultural ombudsman, and development rights purchases to balance tech and farming. Most rural communities lack that infrastructure.

Ronald Hicks, assistant county executive in Dutchess County, describes what took decades to learn: coexistence requires planning before development arrives, not after.[3] Taylor Hubbard of the Virginia Grain Producers' government affairs team argues that farmers themselves need to run for local office — because if they're not at the table, the table moves without them.[1]

Steve Lehr, a managing director at CBRE, notes that well-designed data center projects should benefit everyone — if properly planned.[4] That's a large "if." Most zoning decisions in rural America are made by part-time boards reviewing proposals from billion-dollar companies. The asymmetry is structural.

3
Pattern Confirmation

This is a national pattern accelerated by AI. Data centers consumed approximately 2,000 megawatts of new capacity in 2025 alone, much of it requiring large rural parcels with cheap power and cold air.[2] The Senate introduced legislation in March 2026 to break up meat processing monopolies — signaling broader congressional attention to agricultural economic pressure.[5]

Meanwhile, tariffs continue to batter farm economics. The USDA is dispersing a $12 billion farm bailout. Farmer sentiment has darkened. The fundamental economics of keeping land in agricultural production get harder to defend when a peanut field generates $400 per acre and a data center generates $400,000 per acre in tax value.[6]

Farm Progress published an eight-part investigative series in February 2026 — "Paved Over" — documenting the pattern across multiple states. The series names the actors, maps the conversions, and tracks the governance gaps that let farmland disappear without public deliberation.[1]

Historical Echo Every era of American industrial expansion consumed farmland. Railroads. Highways. Suburbs. The difference now is speed. AI data center demand compresses what was a generational transition into a single planning cycle. Communities that took fifty years to negotiate with industry now have ten — or less. Governance isn't optional. It's the only thing standing between farming and pavement.

When the math is $400 versus $400,000, the field doesn't have a market argument. It has a governance argument. And in most of rural America, that argument hasn't started yet.

Uncertainty The exact pace of farmland conversion varies significantly by state and county. Congressional attention to agricultural economics (meat processing legislation, farm bailout) signals concern but does not guarantee farmland-specific policy responses. Long-term impact of AI data center demand on rural land markets remains uncertain — the industry could consolidate into fewer, larger sites or disperse more widely.
